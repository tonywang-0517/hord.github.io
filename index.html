Abstract

Achieving robust whole-body humanoid control under dynamics mismatch remains a central challenge in robotics. In this work, we present MIRAGE (Masked Imitation for Robotic Action Generation and Execution), a lightweight execution framework that achieves substantially higher success rates under domain shifts, enabling reliable zero-shot sim-to-sim transfer to unseen physics engines where prior methods degrade or fail. Rather than decomposing behaviors into multiple experts or relying on target-domain adapters, MIRAGE employs a single PD control policy operating from standardized sparse commands and a history-conditioned representation.

Our framework introduces two key innovations: SCHEMA (Sparse Command Handling for Embodied Motion Abstraction), a standardized sparse-joint protocol based on SMPL-X keypoints that consolidates fragmented motion sources; and MIRROR (Memory-Integrated Robust Representation for Online Robustness), a history-conditioned control policy that leverages recent state‚Äìaction sequences as implicit dynamics memory, significantly improving stability and sim-to-sim transfer.

Extensive experiments on a Unitree-style G1 humanoid demonstrate that MIRAGE achieves 90.7% success rate in the training simulator (IsaacLab) and 86.0% success rate in zero-shot transfer to an unseen domain (Genesis), significantly outperforming strong baselines including MaskedMimic, OmniH2O, ExBody2, and Hover.

Sim-to-Sim Transfer Excellence

MIRAGE achieves 86.0% success rate in zero-shot transfer to Genesis (unseen domain), outperforming ExBody2 (73.1%) and OmniH2O (72.3%). Under domain randomization, MIRAGE maintains 84.4% success rate while most baselines experience substantial performance degradation.

MIRAGE Framework

MIRAGE is a lightweight execution framework that realizes sparse-to-PD control through a teacher-student architecture. At a high level, MIRAGE learns a single high-frequency policy that maps sparse motion commands (SCHEMA), local observations, and a short history of recent interaction (MIRROR) into PD control targets for a humanoid robot, while remaining robust to changes in dynamics through structured domain randomization.

Two Core Innovations

SCHEMA

Standardized Sparse Command Interface

SCHEMA defines a unified sparse-joint protocol based on SMPL-X keypoints, consolidating fragmented motion sources into a single training and execution format. It enables large-scale humanoid trajectory generation from datasets such as AMASS, where human motion sequences are retargeted to the humanoid, downsampled, and projected onto a set of SMPL-X key joints (e.g., hips, knees, ankles, shoulders, elbows, wrists). This standardized interface decouples perception and planning from low-level actuation, allowing different upstream generators to produce interchangeable SCHEMA packets without modifying the controller or training pipeline.

MIRROR

History-Integrated Dynamics Memory

MIRROR aggregates a short window of past states and actions into a fixed-dimensional history embedding using a QFormer-style memory extractor. Rather than flattening the history through a simple feed-forward encoder, MIRROR performs early fusion between a learned memory-factor prior and the recent interaction trace. The resulting memory token is injected via a residual connection into the latent control representation, enabling the controller to modulate its decisions based on inferred dynamics. This provides domain awareness, allowing the policy to disambiguate which realization of the randomized dynamics it is currently operating in and adjust its control strategy accordingly. Empirically, removing MIRROR significantly degrades sim-to-sim transfer and recovery performance.

Training Methodology

Two-Stage Training Architecture

MIRAGE employs a teacher-student framework to realize sparse-to-PD control. The training process consists of two stages: (1) a goal-conditioned expert policy is trained with reinforcement learning under domain-randomized dynamics, and (2) a compact student policy is distilled from this expert using DAgger-style online imitation, operating directly from SCHEMA-formatted sparse commands and history-conditioned inputs.

Stage 1: Teacher Training

The teacher policy œÄ‚ãÜ is a goal-conditioned reinforcement learning policy trained in simulation under domain-randomized dynamics. It receives the current state st, a dense short-horizon reference motion gfullt retargeted from AMASS, terrain features hterrt, and a history-based embedding of recent states and actions. The teacher is optimized with PPO and generalized advantage estimation (GAE), maximizing a reward that combines kinematic tracking terms, balance and contact consistency penalties, control-effort regularization, and a fall penalty.

Stage 2: Student Distillation

Deploying the teacher directly is impractical since it relies on dense simulator-only references. We therefore distill it into a student policy œÄŒ∏ that operates from sparse SCHEMA commands and history-conditioned inputs. Following a DAgger-style procedure, we iteratively: (1) roll out the current student in the randomized environment with random mask applied to the sparse commands; (2) query the expert on the visited states to obtain target actions and kinematic quantities; (3) update the student to minimize an imitation loss combining position-matching, velocity-matching, root orientation matching, and power regularization terms.

Experimental Setup

  • Robot Platform: Unitree-style G1 humanoid with 29 actuated DoF
  • Control Frequency: 50 Hz PD control
  • Training Simulator: Isaac Lab with contact-rich rigid-body dynamics
  • Evaluation: Zero-shot transfer to Genesis physics engine
  • Motion Dataset: AMASS motion corpus covering locomotion, balance, manipulation-style poses, and recovery motions
  • Evaluation Metrics: Success rate (%), global pose error (Eg-mpjpe), local pose error (Empjpe)

Domain Randomization Strategy

Bridging the Sim-to-Real Gap

Our Domain Randomization (DR) strategy is carefully designed to balance model performance with diverse domain coverage, ensuring robust sim-to-sim and potential sim-to-real transfer. The approach introduces controlled variations across multiple aspects of the simulation environment:

Observation Randomization

Sensor Noise

Slight observation noise is added to sensor readings, simulating real-world measurement uncertainties while maintaining model accuracy.

Physical Parameter Randomization

  • Body Mass: Randomized within a specified range
  • Joint Stiffness: Varied to simulate different actuator characteristics
  • Friction: Contact friction coefficients randomized
  • Damping: Joint damping parameters varied

Actuator Latency Simulation

To mimic real-world hardware delays, joint command signals are probabilistically delayed by 1-2 control steps, simulating:

  • Communication latency
  • Actuator response time
  • Controller processing delays

External Perturbations

Random external forces are applied to the robot at random time intervals, simulating:

  • Physical contacts and collisions
  • Environmental disturbances
  • Unexpected external interactions

Design Philosophy

Our DR configuration strikes a careful balance between two competing objectives: (1) maintaining high task performance in the source domain, and (2) maximizing exposure to diverse input distributions that cover potential target domains. This balanced approach enables MIRAGE to achieve robust generalization across different simulators and physical conditions, as demonstrated in our sim-to-sim transfer experiments.

Training Pipeline

Stage 1: Teacher Training

The teacher policy œÄ‚ãÜ is a goal-conditioned reinforcement learning policy trained in simulation under domain-randomized dynamics. It receives dense short-horizon reference motion from AMASS, terrain features, and history-based embeddings, optimized with PPO and GAE to learn robust dense-to-PD control.

  • Goal-conditioned RL with PPO
  • Domain randomization

Stage 2: Student Distill

A compact student policy œÄŒ∏ is distilled from the expert using DAgger-style online imitation. The student operates directly from SCHEMA-formatted sparse commands with random mask and history-conditioned inputs (MIRROR), learning to match the teacher's robust behavior while exposed to its own state distribution.

  • DAgger-style online distillation
  • SCHEMA sparse command interface with random mask
  • MIRROR history conditioning

Why Does MIRROR Make the Difference?

MIRROR's action-state history provides the robot with "proprioceptive intuition" ‚Äî the ability to sense its own historical states and actions. Without this memory, models trained under DR must use a single fixed policy that attempts to work across all domains but cannot perceive domain-specific characteristics. While this prevents complete failure (as seen in ExBody2 and OmniH2O), it sacrifices optimal performance by lacking the ability to identify the current domain and adapt its strategy accordingly. In contrast, MIRROR enables domain-aware adaptation, allowing the policy to switch to the optimal strategy for each specific domain, achieving strong zero-shot sim-to-sim transfer without any target-domain fine-tuning.

We conduct ablation studies to analyze the contribution of domain randomization (DR) and the MIRROR module under both training and zero-shot testing conditions. The results demonstrate that both components play complementary roles for MIRAGE to achieve robust PD control under cross-domain and perturbed settings.

üí° Key Findings: (1) Removing domain randomization leads to severe performance collapse once test-time perturbations or simulator changes are introduced, confirming DR is essential for robust generalization. (2) Removing MIRROR causes a different failure pattern: performance remains competitive in the training environment (91.3% vs. 90.7%) but degrades sharply under domain shifts, highlighting MIRROR's role as an implicit dynamics memory that allows online adaptation to latent changes in simulator parameters. (3) In the fixed IsaacLab environment, MIRAGE w/o MIRROR achieves slightly higher success (91.3% vs. 90.7%) because without the memory factor, more model capacity can focus on processing other inputs (e.g., future frames). With MIRROR, the per-frame varying memory tokens act as additional noise-like inputs, requiring a larger proportion of model weights to transform dynamic memory factors into fixed "proprioceptive" representations. However, MIRROR is designed to address domain shift problems, and in a fixed, known domain, this mechanism does not provide an advantage and may slightly reduce performance by allocating capacity to unnecessary adaptation mechanisms.

Policy Structure and Generalization Comparison

We compare MIRAGE with existing methods across key dimensions of policy structure and generalization capabilities. Our method uniquely combines a unified expert policy with masked generalization, enabling training with domain randomization and zero-shot transfer to unseen domains. MIRAGE integrates domain-aware adaptation through MIRROR's historical memory, allowing the policy to sense domain-specific dynamics and switch to optimal strategies without explicit domain labels. This comprehensive approach distinguishes MIRAGE from prior work, which typically excels in only a subset of these capabilities.

RISE üîÆ Future Work

Conceptual Design

Recovery via Inferred State Endpoints: Inspired by insights from the MaskedMimic codebase, we hypothesize a future capability where the student model receives future target states (sparse keyframes + temporal offset) as input during distillation. We conjecture this will enable the policy to infer optimal actions that maximize the probability of reaching target states under physical constraints, achieving higher-dimensional recovery mechanisms such as autonomous fall-and-get-up behaviors.

RISE: Vision for Advanced Recovery

Core Hypothesis: The expert model has learned extensive recovery behaviors (e.g., fall-and-get-up) from the training dataset. During online distillation, by modifying the input to include future target states (instead of current states), the student model may develop an emergent ability to reason about action sequences that lead to desired outcomes. This input modification transforms the distillation process from "imitate current actions" to "infer actions toward future goals," potentially enabling:

  • Inverse dynamics reasoning: Computing "what actions now" lead to "target state later"
  • Physics-aware planning: Respecting physical constraints in action inference
  • Complex recovery behaviors: Multi-step recovery like fall detection ‚Üí reorientation ‚Üí stand-up

This represents a promising direction for endowing embodied agents with higher-level recovery intelligence.
